Farm Work

How to find farm work in Australia for your 88 days

Published March 2025 · 10 min read · OzBudget

If you want to extend your Working Holiday Visa for a second year — or even a third — you need to complete 88 days of specified work in regional Australia. For most people this means farm work: fruit picking, vegetable harvesting, pruning, planting, or packing. But the world of regional work is confusing, with dodgy operators, underpayment, and paperwork traps that can leave your days not counting toward the 88.

Key rule: Not all regional work counts. The work must be in an eligible industry AND in an eligible postcode. Always check the ATO's postcode checker before committing to a job.

What work qualifies?

Eligible industries include: plant and animal cultivation (fruit picking, pruning, planting, harvesting), fishing and pearling, tree farming and felling, mining, and construction in a regional area. Tourism and hospitality in regional areas also qualify for the subclass 462 visa but not for 417.

The easiest option for most people is fruit and vegetable picking, which is available year-round in different regions of Australia. Demand varies by season and crop — cherries in November-December, apples in February-April, mangoes in July-September.

Where to look for work

  • Harvest Trail (jobsearch.gov.au) — the Australian Government's own job board for harvest work. Free, legitimate, and the best starting point.
  • Seek.com.au — search for "fruit picking", "farm hand", or "harvest worker" in regional areas.
  • Facebook Groups — "Working Holiday Australia", "Backpackers Australia", and city/region-specific groups are active and useful for real-time intel on which farms are hiring.
  • Hostel noticeboards — especially in regional towns like Mildura, Shepparton, Bundaberg, and Carnarvon. Hostel owners often have relationships with local farms.
  • Labour hire companies — companies like Agri Labour Australia and Atlam Group place workers on farms. They handle paperwork and transport, but take a cut.

How to avoid getting ripped off

Wage theft in the agricultural sector is a known problem in Australia. Before starting any farm work job:

  • Confirm you'll be paid at least the award minimum — the Horticulture Award sets specific piece rates and a minimum fallback hourly rate
  • Ask for a written contract or letter of engagement before you start
  • Check that the farm is in an eligible postcode at ato.gov.au/eligiblework
  • Get your employer to sign Form 1263 — keep copies of everything
  • Never pay a "job placement fee" to get a farm job — this is a scam

Counting your days correctly

Days are counted as calendar days, not working days. If you work Monday to Friday, weekends count too. You must work for the same employer for at least one day per week to have those weeks count. Keep your payslips, bank statements showing payments, and employment records.

Tip: Take photos of your time at the farm, keep a private record of your days worked with dates, and follow up with your employer regularly to ensure your Form 1263 is accurate and up to date.

The financial reality

Farm work often pays less per hour than city hospitality work, but living costs in regional areas can be dramatically lower. Free or cheap accommodation on-farm is common, food costs less, and there's simply less to spend money on. Many WHV holders find they save more money during their regional stint than they do in the city.

Banking

Best bank accounts for Working Holiday Visa holders in Australia

Published March 2025 · 7 min read · OzBudget

Getting your banking set up correctly on arrival saves you hundreds in fees and significantly better exchange rates when sending money home. Here's what actually works for WHV holders in 2025.

Australian bank accounts — the top picks

ING Orange Everyday is a favourite for many WHV holders. It has no monthly fees, reimburses ATM fees nationwide, and gives you a fee-free international debit card once you deposit $1,000/month. The app is excellent and BPAY is supported for rent payments. Opening takes 10 minutes online after arriving.

Up Bank is a digital-only bank (backed by Bendigo Bank) that's popular for its clean app, instant transaction notifications, and smart savings features. It takes 5 minutes to open, requires no minimum balance, and has no monthly fee. The app makes it easy to split your money into savings "savers" (sub-accounts) for rent, taxes, and travel funds.

Commonwealth Bank is the largest bank in Australia and has the widest ATM network. Worth considering if you'll be working in very regional areas where other bank ATMs are scarce. Monthly fee ($4–6/month) but easy to waive with regular deposits.

What to bring on arrival: Your passport, visa grant letter, and a local Australian address (even a hostel address works for the first account). Most banks can open accounts with a temporary address — just update it once you have a permanent one.

Sending money home — don't use your bank

Australian banks charge 3–5% in hidden currency conversion fees on international transfers. On a $2,000 transfer that's $60–100 lost to fees. Use a dedicated transfer service instead:

  • Wise (formerly TransferWise) — mid-market exchange rate with a small transparent fee (~0.5–1%). The best option for most currencies. Set up before you leave home.
  • Revolut — excellent for multi-currency spending and cheap transfers. Has free ATM withdrawals up to a monthly limit. Good for travel between countries.
  • OFX — better for large transfers (above $5,000). No transfer fee on large amounts, competitive rates.

Travel cards worth having

A Wise card gives you a debit card linked to your Wise account with real exchange rates, no foreign transaction fees, and the ability to hold multiple currencies. Useful if you're travelling across Southeast Asia before or after Australia.

A Revolut card similarly provides interbank exchange rates. The free tier has ATM limits; the paid tier (£4.99/month) removes these.

Tax and your bank account

Your Australian bank account will ask for your Tax File Number (TFN). You don't have to provide it to your bank — but providing it avoids them withholding tax on any interest earned in savings accounts. With a TFN on file, you pay tax at your marginal rate; without it, you're withheld at 47%. Given how low interest rates are, the impact is small, but worth providing.

Super & Tax

How to claim your super back when you leave Australia

Published March 2025 · 8 min read · OzBudget

Every working holiday maker who works for an Australian employer accumulates superannuation — the Australian retirement savings system. Your employer is legally required to pay 11.5% of your ordinary earnings into a super fund on top of your wage. When you leave Australia permanently, you can claim all of it back as a Departing Australia Superannuation Payment (DASP).

Heads up: The DASP tax rate for WHV holders is 65% — significantly higher than for Australian residents. Despite this, it's still real money worth claiming. Don't leave it.

How much super will I have?

At 11.5%, if you earn $50,000 during your time in Australia, your employer will have contributed approximately $5,750 to your super. After DASP tax at 65%, you'd receive around $2,012. Smaller than it looks, but it adds up — and many WHV holders have multiple thousands sitting unclaimed.

Use the Superannuation tab on OzBudget to calculate your exact accrued super and expected DASP payout based on your income and weeks worked.

Step-by-step: how to claim DASP

  • Step 1 — Leave Australia: Your visa must have expired or been cancelled, and you must have left Australia permanently (or not hold a current visa).
  • Step 2 — Wait 6 months: The ATO requires you to wait at least 6 months after your visa expires before applying. This is non-negotiable.
  • Step 3 — Apply online: Go to ato.gov.au/DASP and complete the online application. You'll need your Australian super fund details, TFN, and passport.
  • Step 4 — Wait for payment: Processing takes 28 days. The ATO contacts your super fund, who sends the payment directly to your overseas bank account (minus the 65% DASP tax).

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Not providing your TFN to your super fund — without it, contributions are taxed at 45% going in, not 15%, reducing your final balance
  • Multiple super funds — if you changed jobs, you may have super in multiple funds. Search for all your accounts at ato.gov.au/myGov before leaving
  • Leaving it too long — unclaimed super eventually gets transferred to the ATO as "lost super". You can still claim it but the process is more complex
  • Applying before 6 months — applications submitted before the 6-month waiting period will be rejected

Can I transfer super to my home country pension?

Not directly. DASP is a cash payment to your bank account, not a pension transfer. There's no mechanism to roll your Australian super into a foreign retirement account. The payment arrives as a lump sum minus the 65% withholding tax.

Money Tips

How to actually save money on a Working Holiday Visa in Australia

Published March 2025 · 9 min read · OzBudget

Australia is expensive. Sydney and Melbourne consistently rank in the world's top 10 most expensive cities. But thousands of WHV holders leave with significant savings every year — the difference is habits, housing, and knowing where not to waste money.

The biggest cost: housing

Rent is your largest expense by far — typically 40–60% of your total monthly spend. In Sydney the median room in a shared house is $350–500/week. In Brisbane or Perth it's $280–380/week. In regional areas it can be $100–200/week or even included with farm work.

  • Join Facebook groups like "Flatmates — Sydney" or use flatmates.com.au to find house shares
  • Avoid living in backpacker hostels for more than 2–3 weeks — the daily rate is usually $35–55/night which works out to $1,000–1,500/month
  • Arrive with a short-term plan and move to a house share as quickly as possible
  • Living closer to the city costs more but can save on transport costs — model the total

Food: the biggest variable expense

Eating out in Australia is expensive — a pub meal is $25–35, a café breakfast is $18–25, a coffee is $5–6. If you eat out every day you'll spend $800–1,200/month on food alone. Cooking at home brings this to $300–450/month.

  • Aldi and Lidl (where available) are significantly cheaper than Woolworths and Coles for staples
  • Farmer's markets at end-of-day often sell surplus produce cheaply
  • Meal prep on Sundays — batch cooking saves both money and time during the week
  • The weekly grocery budget for one person eating well at home is $80–120

Tax: the silent leak

Many WHV holders don't think carefully about tax until they've already lost money. Key habits:

  • Get your TFN immediately on arrival — without it your employer takes 45% of everything
  • Track your tax withheld on every payslip — use our Payslip Check tab to verify your employer is deducting correctly
  • Lodge your tax return every year even if you've left Australia — many people are owed refunds
  • Keep receipts for work-related expenses (uniforms, tools, travel between job sites) — these are deductible

The 50-30-20 rule adapted for WHV

A simple framework that works well in Australia: spend no more than 50% of net income on needs (rent, food, transport, utilities), 20% on wants (going out, travel, experiences), and save at least 30%.

On a $950/week net income in Sydney: $475 on needs, $190 on fun, and $285 saved. Over 12 months that's $14,820 saved — enough to travel Southeast Asia for 3–4 months, fund the flight home, or start something back home.

The real secret: The biggest financial difference between WHV holders who leave with savings and those who don't isn't income — it's whether they tracked their spending. Use a budget tracker from day one.
